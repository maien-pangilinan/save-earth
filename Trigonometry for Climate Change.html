How can we use Trigonometry in Climate Change related problems?

You might ask “What does Trigonometry have to do with climate change?” I have to admit, trigonometry was quite hard to relate to this subject, but to my surprise, it is quite easy to think of certain problems that involve trigonometry and because of this, I realized that Trigonometry is everywhere. In this web page, you will encounter different problems about climate change that relate to trigonometric functions.

Satellites

In 1995, the first weather satellite was launched and ever since, Satellites have become an essential part of the climate change mission. It is simply impossible to measure climate change without the use of satellite data. Environmentalists and scientists will be unable to evaluate, assess, and anticipate the consequences of climate change without the accurate data and other inputs offered by satellites, and policymakers would be unable to devise effective measures.

More than half of the 50 critical climate change factors can be determined using satellite data. Satellite radar altimetry, which measures the distance between a satellite and the earth's surface and provides exact information about sea levels, is one of these insights. Satellites are also used to measure the chemical makeup of the atmosphere and greenhouse gases such as methane. There are currently roughly 162 satellites in orbit that measure various climate change indicators.

Melting of Ice

A large fraction of Earth’s freshwater is frozen: It’s stored in glaciers all around the world, in both the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets . Due to Global warming, these glaciers and ice sheets melt causing the oceans and sea levels to rise.

Sea Levels

As a result of global warming, glaciers and ice sheets are melting, causing sea levels to rise. Sea levels are rising at a rate of a few millimeters per year. While this may appear insignificant at first look, it can result in major inland seawater incursion over time, particularly in low-lying coastal locations.
